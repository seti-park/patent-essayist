# Figure selection — US 12,560,948 B2 (investor moat article)

Three figures carry the argument. The full filing has eight figure sheets in total, including a
multi-panel kneel sequence and two system block diagrams; the rest are supporting detail not
needed for a moat read.

| Figure | Role in the article | Why selected |
|---|---|---|
| **FIG. 1** | The subject: a humanoid mobile robot (`100`) with torso, arms (`110`), legs (`112`), end effectors (`114`), feet (`116`). | Establishes the body the safety problem is about: tall, top-heavy, dynamically balanced. The shape is the reason a simple power-cut is dangerous. |
| **FIG. 3** | The claimed logic: a decision tree from "should I decelerate?" through "is it safe to reconfigure?" to "safe operating stop," with hazard information feeding each gate, hold states (stand and wait, kneel and wait), and a time axis. | This is the moat in one image. It shows the escalation and the clearance check that claim 1 locks, not a single stop threshold. |
| **FIG. 6** | The end state: the robot in a compact kneel, knees and end effectors on the ground, center-of-gravity dropped. | Shows what "lowering a center-of-gravity" buys: a low, predictable, compact posture that collapses safely on a fault. The payoff of the claimed reconfiguration. |

Selected figure set: **1, 3, 6**. All three are referenced in the draft; no other figure
numbers are referenced (keeps the figure-use gate clean).
