Anthropic's Recorder: The Blueprint for Software That Finally Learns from You.

Forget the moat question for a minute. Strip this patent down to its idea and it turns into one of the most quietly optimistic documents in AI. It describes a machine that learns to do your work by watching you do it, and by listening to why. Anthropic now holds it, through the Adept patent family it picked up in 2025. Here is the fun version of the story: not whether it is defensible, but what it could become.

## Show, Don't Tell

The core of WO2025199330 is a "recorder." It sits quietly between you and an app while you work. It captures the screen before you act, the action you take, and, the part most people miss, your own notes on intent: what you were trying to do, a hint when something is off, the task in your own words [00374]. Then it packages that into a training example: here was the situation, here was the move a human made [00372].

That is teaching by demonstration. It is how people have always passed on a craft, by showing rather than describing, and the patent turns it into something a model can actually learn from. Adept said the quiet part out loud in the spec: the goal is "a universal collaborator for every knowledge worker," with people "in the driver's seat" [0017].

## Why It Is Bigger Than It Looks

The thing holding agents back was never ambition. It was data. Nobody can hand-write instructions for "how to use every piece of software on earth." But anyone can show. Every time a person does a task with the recorder on, the system earns a fresh, grounded example, and it records the why behind the what. A machine that learns from intent, not just from clicks, is a different kind of machine.

## What You Could Build on It

Now the good part. Picture where this goes once it works well.

- **Personal automation for everyone.** Show an agent a task once and it handles it from then on. The dull 30 percent of your job quietly disappears, and you keep the part you actually like.
- **Software that offers to help.** Apps that notice the repetitive thing you keep doing and ask, "want me to take this one?" The interface stops being a wall of menus and starts being a partner.
- **Agents that read intent.** Because the data pairs a goal with an action, you can train agents that infer what you are after and ask the one smart clarifying question instead of guessing. Imagine pairing that data with a model line already praised for grasping what users mean, the kind of thing people rave about in Fable 5. That is a thrilling combination to think about.
- **Real accessibility.** Anyone who finds a mouse and keyboard hard gets an agent that acts on their behalf from a simple demonstration.
- **A teaching flywheel.** Every demonstration makes every agent a little better, so humans and machines compound each other instead of competing.
- **The same idea crosses into the physical world.** Watch a human, learn the action. That is the through-line from self-driving to Optimus to software agents, one bet that perception plus demonstration beats hand-coded rules everywhere.

## Why Anthropic Is the Right Home

A recorder is only as good as the model and the craft behind it, which is exactly why this landing spot is interesting. Put this data recipe next to frontier models, real compute, a team that already obsesses over what users actually mean, and some of the very people who invented it, including Adept's co-founder and CTO who went to Anthropic. The dream stops being a pitch deck and starts being a roadmap. The recorder is the data engine. The models are the engine it feeds.

## The Flip

For thirty years we adapted to software, to its menus and shortcuts and quirks. This patent points at the opposite world, where software adapts to us by watching how we work and learning what we want. It is a small patent with a long horizon. The question worth getting excited about is not whether anyone can copy it. It is what someone ambitious decides to build with it.
