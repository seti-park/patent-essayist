Adept Raised $415M to Build AI Agents. The Moat Left with the People.

Adept looked like a frontier-AI winner. It raised $415M at a $1B valuation, with Microsoft, Nvidia, and Workday on the cap table, to build agents that use software the way people do. Its patent portfolio is real and partly granted. So does the company have a technical moat? For an investor, the honest answer is no, and the patent itself shows why.

## What Adept Actually Patented

The flagship filing, WO2025199330 (granted in the US as US12437238), does not claim a magic model. Its 20 claims cover a "recorder": an intermediary that sits between you and an app, intercepts your clicks, snapshots the screen state, and turns the human demonstration into agent training data [00372]. Specific and clever. But it fences a mechanism, not an advantage. It is also a training-process claim, so you usually cannot tell from the outside whether a rival trained the same way. Low detectability means weak enforcement.

## Where Moats Actually Live

In agentic AI, the durable edge is not a describable mechanism. It is the proprietary data flywheel, the post-training craft, and the team. None of that is patentable, and all of it walked out the door. In 2024 Amazon hired Adept's leadership and took a non-exclusive license, and investors roughly recouped principal with no upside. Co-founder and CTO Niki Parmar went to Anthropic. By early 2026, even Amazon's David Luan had left to start something new. A patent on the data engine does not fence the fuel.

## The Paradigm Is Moving

The approach the patent protects, pixel-level visual automation learned from recorded demos, is the one the market is leaving. Google shut down Project Mariner in May 2026 and routed its agents through structured APIs, not screenshots, citing reliability and cost. The same vision-to-action idea now thrives in robotics, from Tesla Optimus to the Tesla and xAI "Digital Optimus," precisely because the physical world has no API. Software does, so the digital version is the one getting designed around. And everyone arrived here in parallel: imitation learning is a decades-old idea, not an Adept invention. Convergence this broad is the opposite of an exclusive position.

## The Verdict

Strong patent craftsmanship, weak investable moat. Treat the portfolio as a defensive, cross-licensing asset inside a bigger lab, not the base of a standalone franchise. The outcome was the tell: when your moat is people and data, you get acqui-hired, not compounded. $415M in, a license out, and the advantage left with the engineers.

One open item for diligence: confirm who owns the family now. The license to Amazon is on record; a 2025 assignment to Anthropic shows up in patent-data sources but still needs USPTO confirmation.
